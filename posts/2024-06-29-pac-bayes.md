## Why This Topic?

If you’re already familiar with Bayesian inference and have experience with high-dimensional modeling, PAC-Bayes theory offers a compelling extension of your toolset. It combines the statistical rigor of **Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning** with **Bayesian reasoning** to provide **tight generalization guarantees**—especially valuable in modern deep learning, where traditional bounds (like VC dimension) often become vacuous.

PAC-Bayes is particularly useful when quantifying **model uncertainty**, justifying **ensembles**, interpreting **dropout**, and training **Bayesian neural networks**. It bridges the theoretical and practical in a way that makes it immediately applicable to real-world modeling.

---

## 1. Motivation and Intuition

In traditional supervised learning, especially under the PAC framework, we want to learn a hypothesis that generalizes well beyond the training set. Classical generalization bounds often involve:

- Worst-case complexity measures (VC dimension, Rademacher complexity)
- Uniform convergence arguments

But these bounds:

- Can be loose in high dimensions
- Don’t incorporate prior knowledge
- Focus only on a single hypothesis

**PAC-Bayes reframes this**:

> Rather than choosing a single hypothesis, we consider a **distribution over hypotheses**—a posterior over models—and bound the **expected generalization error**.

The result is a bound that:

- Reflects how well the posterior fits the data
- Penalizes deviation from the prior (via KL divergence)
- Holds with high probability over the draw of the dataset

This is particularly powerful in settings where we work with **stochastic models**—such as dropout networks, Bayesian neural nets, variational approximations, or ensembles.

---

## 2. PAC-Bayes Bound: High-Level Formulation

Let’s formalize it. Consider:

- Hypothesis space: \(\mathcal{H}\)
- Prior over hypotheses (independent of data): \(P\)
- Posterior over hypotheses (data-dependent): \(Q\)
- Training set: \(S = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n\)
- Loss: \(\ell(h, x, y) \in [0, 1]\) (bounded)
- True risk: \(L(h) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}[\ell(h(x), y)]\)
- Empirical risk: \(\hat{L}_S(h) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \ell(h(x_i), y_i)\)

### **Classic PAC-Bayes Bound**

With probability at least \(1 - \delta\), over samples \(S \sim \mathcal{D}^n\), the following holds for all posteriors \(Q\):

\[
\mathbb{E}_{h \sim Q}[L(h)] \leq \mathbb{E}_{h \sim Q}[\hat{L}_S(h)] + \sqrt{ \frac{ \text{KL}(Q || P) + \log\left(\frac{2\sqrt{n}}{\delta}\right) }{2n} }
\]

### Interpretation:

- **First term**: expected empirical risk under \(Q\)
- **Second term**: complexity penalty measuring deviation from the prior
- **KL divergence** \(\text{KL}(Q || P)\): how much we learn from the data

## 3. Worked Example: Bayesian Neural Network with Dropout

Suppose you’re training a binary classifier using a neural network and use **dropout** at training time. Dropout induces stochasticity—a distribution over models.

- Let prior \(P = \mathcal{N}(0, I)\)
- Approximate posterior \(Q = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)\) learned via variational inference or dropout

Then:

- You can **sample** weights \(w \sim Q\) to estimate \(\mathbb{E}_Q[\hat{L}_S(h)]\)
- Compute \(\text{KL}(Q || P)\) in closed form (if Gaussian)
- Plug into the PAC-Bayes bound

This process has been applied to:

- **Sparse NNs** (compression)
- **Ensembles** (viewing ensemble as posterior)
- **Flat minima** (flat regions in loss have small KL)

---

## 4. The Role of the Prior: Is the Bound Prior-Free?

Only **formally**. The bound **depends on the prior** through \(\text{KL}(Q || P)\)—but it doesn’t require the prior elsewhere.

Still, choosing \(P\) is critical. Three common approaches:

### a. Classic (Data-Independent) Priors

- Fixed before seeing any data
- \(P = \mathcal{N}(0, I)\), uniform over \(\mathcal{H}\), etc.

* Carefully use surrogate or unlabeled data to define \(P\)
* Not strictly prior-free, but still yields valid bounds

### c. Data-Dependent Priors

- Breaks theory (bound no longer guaranteed)
- In practice: useful and often accurate

### Why KL Is Central

- KL is a penalty for **learning** from data
- If \(Q \approx P\), you didn’t learn much → low penalty
- If \(Q\) diverges from \(P\), you must pay more

Example: If both \(P, Q\) are Gaussian:

\[
\text{KL}(Q \parallel P) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i \left( \mu_i^2 + \sigma_i^2 - 1 - \log \sigma_i^2 \right)
\]

If \(\mu_i \approx 0\), \(\sigma_i \approx 1\): KL is small

## 5. Proof Sketch (Heavier Math)

Goal: Prove that with high probability:

\[
\mathbb{E}_{h \sim Q}[L(h)] \leq \mathbb{E}_{h \sim Q}[\hat{L}_S(h)] + \sqrt{ \frac{ \text{KL}(Q || P) + \log(1/\delta) }{2n} }
\]

### Step 1: Hoeffding's Inequality

For any fixed \(h\), since \(\ell \in [0,1]\):

\[
\mathbb{E}_S\left[\exp\left(\lambda (L(h) - \hat{L}_S(h))\right)\right] \leq \exp\left(\frac{\lambda^2}{8n}\right)
\]

Use **Donsker-Varadhan** inequality:

\[
\mathbb{E}_{h \sim Q}[f(h)] \leq \text{KL}(Q || P) + \log \mathbb{E}_{h \sim P}[e^{f(h)}]
\]

Set \(f(h) = \lambda(L(h) - \hat{L}_S(h))\):

\[
\lambda (\mathbb{E}_Q[L(h)] - \mathbb{E}_Q[\hat{L}_S(h)]) \leq \text{KL}(Q || P) + \log \mathbb{E}_P\left[ e^{\lambda(L(h) - \hat{L}_S(h))} \right]
\]

Apply expectation over \(S\) and Hoeffding again:

\[
\mathbb{E}_S\left[\log \mathbb{E}_{h \sim P}[e^{\lambda(L(h) - \hat{L}_S(h))}]\right] \leq \frac{\lambda^2}{8n}
\]

### Step 3: Combine and Rearrange

With high probability:

\[
\mathbb{E}_Q[L(h)] \leq \mathbb{E}_Q[\hat{L}_S(h)] + \frac{1}{\lambda}\left(\text{KL}(Q \parallel P) + \log(1/\delta) + \frac{\lambda^2}{8n}\right)
\]

Choose \(\lambda = \sqrt{\frac{8n}{\text{KL}(Q \parallel P) + \log(1/\delta)}}\), plug in, get the final form:

\[
\mathbb{E}_Q[L(h)] \leq \mathbb{E}_Q[\hat{L}_S(h)] + \sqrt{ \frac{ \text{KL}(Q || P) + \log(1/\delta) }{2n} }
\]

## 6. Summary and Takeaways

- PAC-Bayes gives **probabilistic generalization guarantees** for distributions over hypotheses
- It blends **Bayesian learning** with **frequentist guarantees**
- The bound penalizes deviation from the prior: more learning = looser bound unless you have more data
- KL divergence acts as a **compression penalty** and controls generalization
- Particularly useful for:
  - Dropout networks
  - Ensembles
  - Bayesian neural networks
  - Learning flat minima
